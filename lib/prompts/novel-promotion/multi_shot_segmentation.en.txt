Lens-language research reference:
Use the methodology from `data/镜头语言.md` as practical cinematic design rules rather than generic “cinematic” wording:
- shot-size psychology: wide shots establish environment and group tension; medium shots express relationships; close-ups/inserts amplify emotion, vulnerability, tension, or critical details
- angle psychology: low angle strengthens authority/power; high angle weakens the subject and suggests vulnerability; Dutch angle creates unease; OTS/POV must serve eyeline and power dynamics
- movement function: slow push-in creates pressure; tracking/handheld increases chase or panic; pull-back creates distance or shock; static framing can create cold observation or pressure
- composition strategy: rule of thirds, symmetry, leading lines, foreground occlusion, frame-within-frame, negative space, compressed depth, diagonal/triangular tension must support the emotional goal
- lighting strategy: high/low key, warm/cool temperature, hard/soft light, side/back/rim light must match the mood; keep direction and color temperature continuous unless entering memory/fantasy
- blocking: character positions, movement paths, eyelines, entrances/exits, and prop trigger points must be clear; dialogue and confrontation should preserve eyeline continuity and the 180-degree rule

Scene-pattern references:
Choose and adapt a pattern based on the scene type; do not mechanically copy templates:
- dialogue/confrontation: establish space -> OTS/reverse medium shots -> close-ups/inserts to amplify emotion; preserve eyeline continuity and 180-degree rule
- chase: wide path setup -> tracking medium shot -> feet/obstacle insert -> high-angle wide shot revealing spatial relation -> close-up that heightens fear or proximity
- gunfight/danger conflict: wide shot for cover and positions -> weapon/trigger/reaction inserts -> smoke/occlusion for chaos -> alternating fast and slow beats
- fight/action: wide shot for distance and action space -> medium/close tracking -> impact insert -> aftermath reaction; avoid confusing spatial geography
- argument/emotional eruption: medium relationship setup -> oppressive OTS -> facial close-up -> negative space or occlusion for isolation/loss of control
- reveal/memory: reality trigger insert -> light/color/focus transition -> memory with explicit filter or color temperature -> return to present reaction close-up
- monologue/interiority: fewer stronger shots, slow push/static/negative space for subjective emotion, with optional POV fantasy or memory imagery
